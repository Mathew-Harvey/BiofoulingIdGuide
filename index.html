Quick reference for divers cleaning ship hulls in WA - know what you're scraping off

What's This Guide For?

The Job

You're cleaning hulls. You need to spot the bad stuff before you scrape it off. This guide shows you what to look for and what to report.

Why It Matters

Marine pests hitch rides on hulls. Some nasty ones have already turned up in WA - like carpet sea squirt at HMAS Stirling. Divers are our first line of defence.

Priority Levels

  • HIGH Stop work. Report now.
  • MEDIUM Report within 24 hours
  • LOW Note it in your dive report

  • STOP cleaning in that area
  • Take photos - close-up + wide shot, use your hand for scale
  • Note where on the hull (bow distance, depth, port/starboard)
  • DON'T scrape it off until you've reported it
  • Surface and tell your supervisor

Part 1: Invasive Hull Foulers ‚Äî The Bad Stuff

These are the pests that stick to hulls and need reporting. If you see any of these, follow your reporting protocol.

Carpet Sea Squirt
Didemnum vexillum
HIGH
Sea Squirt

Nicknamed "sea vomit" - and it looks like it. Creamy-tan carpet that grows over everything. Already found at HMAS Stirling and Henderson.

How to Spot It

  • Creamy/tan/orange colour - like old wax
  • Veiny look - like varicose veins
  • Feels leathery, NOT slimy
  • Can drip down like melted candle wax
  • Tiny holes (siphons) all over surface
Don't confuse with: Native colonial sea squirts - those are thinner, more gelatinous, and don't have the veiny/waxy texture.
White Colonial Sea Squirt
Didemnum perlucidum
HIGH
Sea Squirt

Similar to carpet sea squirt but white/see-through. Forms thin sheets that spread over hull surfaces. Already in WA waters.

How to Spot It

  • White or see-through
  • Thin sheet - hugs the surface
  • Smooth, not lumpy
  • Can see tiny dots (zooids) inside
Asian Green Mussel
Perna viridis
HIGH
Mussel

Big mussel with bright green shell. Common on ships coming from Asia. Check sea chests carefully - they love them.

How to Spot It

  • Young ones = bright emerald green
  • Older ones = brownish-green
  • Big - 80-165mm (hand-sized)
  • Inside is blue-green when open
  • Beak points down
Don't confuse with: Blue mussel (native) - those are blue-black, not green.
Black-striped False Mussel
Mytilopsis sallei
HIGH
Mussel

Small stripy mussel that forms thick clusters. Loves brackish water so check sea chests and anywhere with mixed water.

How to Spot It

  • Small - only up to 25mm (thumbnail-sized)
  • Dark stripes on shell
  • Packs together in thick layers
  • Often found inside sea chests
Brown Mussel
Perna perna
MEDIUM
Mussel

Heavy-duty fouler from Africa/South America. Forms thick mats that are hard to scrape.

How to Spot It

  • Dark brown to black shell
  • Up to 90mm (fist-sized)
  • Builds up in dense clumps
  • Thick shell, hard to break
NZ Green-lipped Mussel
Perna canaliculus
MEDIUM
Mussel

Big mussel from NZ. Look for vessels that have been to New Zealand recently.

How to Spot It

  • Green "lip" around shell edge - key ID feature
  • Brown-green shell outside
  • Massive - up to 240mm (bigger than your hand)
Pacific Oyster
Magallana gigas
MEDIUM
Oyster

Big gnarly oyster that cements itself on. Sharp edges - watch your gloves. Glues itself on solid so hard to remove in one piece.

How to Spot It

  • Messy irregular shell - no neat shape
  • Can get huge - up to 400mm
  • Rough frilly edges - sharp!
  • Glued flat to the surface
European Fan Worm
Sabella spallanzanii
HIGH
Tube Worm

Big feathery worm in a leathery tube. The fan is much bigger than native tube worms. Already established in WA.

How to Spot It

  • Fan is HUGE - 45-60mm across
  • Fan spirals in (not flat like natives)
  • Stripy - orange/purple/white bands
  • Tube is brown and leathery
  • Fan pulls in when you get close
Don't confuse with: Native serpulid worms - those have smaller white/pink fans and white hard tubes.
Stalked Sea Squirt
Styela clava
MEDIUM
Sea Squirt

Looks like a brown pickle on a stalk. Leathery and wrinkly. Stands up off the hull surface.

How to Spot It

  • Club-shaped body on a stalk
  • Brown/tan colour
  • Leathery and wrinkled like a prune
  • 50-160mm tall
  • Stands alone, not in colonies
Pleated Sea Squirt
Styela plicata
MEDIUM
Sea Squirt

Looks like a grey-brown golf ball with ridges. Common on hulls worldwide.

How to Spot It

  • Ridged/pleated surface like a brain
  • Grey-brown colour
  • Up to 70mm (golf ball sized)
  • Sits alone, not in colonies
Vase Tunicate
Ciona intestinalis
MEDIUM
Sea Squirt

Soft squishy tube like a see-through pickle. Yellowish-green and wobbly when touched.

How to Spot It

  • See-through yellowish-green
  • Tube/vase shaped
  • Two openings at top (siphons)
  • Soft and squishy - not hard
  • Up to 150mm tall
Japanese Kelp (Wakame)
Undaria pinnatifida
MEDIUM
Kelp

Big brown seaweed with a central spine. The stuff they put in miso soup. Already in southern Australia.

How to Spot It

  • Big brown fronds with a midrib spine
  • Wavy ruffled edges
  • Can be up to 3m long
  • Frilly reproductive bit at the base
Killer Algae
Caulerpa taxifolia
HIGH
Algae

Bright green feathery algae. Called "killer algae" because it smothers everything. Can hitchhike on anchor chains too.

How to Spot It

  • Bright green - stands out
  • Feather-shaped fronds
  • Spreads with creeping runners
  • Can get 65cm tall

Part 2: Niche Area Hitchhikers

These aren't foulers - they don't grow on the hull. But they hide in sea chests, rope guards, gratings and other nooks. You might find them when inspecting those areas.

European Shore Crab
Carcinus maenas
MEDIUM
Crab

Green crab that hides in sea chests and rope guards. Already an established pest in Australia but still report sightings.

How to Spot It

  • Green shell (can be orange underneath)
  • 5 spines each side behind the eyes
  • Up to 90mm across
  • Check sea chests and niche areas
Asian Paddle Crab
Charybdis japonica
MEDIUM
Crab

Swimming crab with paddle-shaped back legs. Can shelter in niche areas on vessels.

How to Spot It

  • Back legs are flat paddles (for swimming)
  • 6 spines between the eyes
  • Up to 120mm across
  • Olive-brown colour

Part 3: Native Hull Fouling ‚Äî The Normal Stuff

This is what you'll see most of the time. It's the normal WA marine growth. Knowing it helps you spot when something's wrong.

Blue Mussel
Mytilus galloprovincialis
Mussel

Your everyday Aussie mussel. Blue-black shell in clumps. You'll scrape heaps of these.

How to Spot It

  • Blue-black to dark brown shell
  • Pointy triangle shape
  • Clumps together with threads
  • NOT green - that's the Asian one
Acorn Barnacles
Balanidae family
Barnacle

The classic white barnacle cones. All over hulls, props, sea chests. Everywhere.

How to Spot It

  • White/grey cone shapes
  • Diamond-shaped opening at top
  • Glued directly to surface
  • Can be packed solid
Large Red Barnacles
Megabalanus spp.
Barnacle

Big pink/red barnacles. Same deal as the white ones but bigger and coloured.

How to Spot It

  • Big cones - up to 50mm across
  • Red/pink colour
  • Ridged shell plates
Serpulid Tube Worms
Serpulidae & Spirorbidae
Tube Worm

White calcareous tubes with small feathery fans. Tiny spiral ones (spirorbids) and bigger straight ones.

How to Spot It

  • Hard white tubes (calcium)
  • Spirorbids are tiny coils - 3mm
  • Larger serpulids are straight/curved
  • Small feathery fan - NOT the big spiral fan of European fan worm
Sydney Coral / Tube Worm
Galeolaria caespitosa
Tube Worm

White tube worm that builds up in reef-like masses. Pink feathery tentacles.

How to Spot It

  • White calcified tubes in clumps
  • Builds up in reef-like masses
  • Pink feeding tentacles
Bushy Bryozoans
Bugula spp.
Bryozoan

Feathery purple/brown tufts. Looks like a fuzzy bush or seaweed but it's actually tiny animals.

How to Spot It

  • Purple/brown colour
  • Bushy branching shape
  • Delicate and feathery
  • Flexible - waves in current
Red-rust Bryozoan
Watersipora spp.
Bryozoan

Flat orangey-red crust that spreads over surfaces. Very common hull fouler.

How to Spot It

  • Red/rust/orange sheets
  • Flat encrusting growth
  • Dark black edge where it's growing
  • Hard calcified surface
Chalky Bryozoans
Celleporaria spp.
Bryozoan

White/pink lumpy crusts. Looks like someone splattered plaster on the hull.

How to Spot It

  • White/pink colour
  • Chalky texture
  • Lumpy irregular growth
  • Hard surface
Encrusting Sponges
Demospongiae
Sponge

Soft spongy sheets in various colours. Orange, yellow, grey. Has tiny holes all over.

How to Spot It

  • Sheet or cushion growth
  • Various colours
  • Soft and spongy
  • Tiny pores visible
Native Colonial Sea Squirts
Didemnidae (native)
Sea Squirt

Thin jelly-like mats. Similar to the invasive Didemnum but thinner and more gelatinous. Be careful with ID.

How to Spot It

  • Thin gelatinous mats
  • Various colours
  • Thinner than invasive carpet sea squirt
  • No veiny/waxy texture
Could be confused with: Carpet sea squirt (invasive) - if in doubt, report it. The invasive one is thicker, veiny, and feels leathery not slimy.
Solitary Sea Squirts (Native)
Cnemidocarpa spp.
Sea Squirt

Single blob-shaped animals, orange or white. Two holes on top (siphons).

How to Spot It

  • Orange/white colour
  • Single blobs, not colonies
  • Two openings on top
  • Leathery skin
Sea Lettuce
Ulva australis
Algae

Bright green thin sheets. Looks like salad leaves. Common in nutrient-rich ports.

How to Spot It

  • Bright green
  • Thin see-through sheets
  • Ruffled wavy edges
  • Soft like lettuce
Green Hair Algae
Cladophora spp.
Algae

Stringy green filaments in tangled mats. Like green hair or cotton wool.

How to Spot It

  • Bright green filaments
  • Branching threads
  • Tangled mats
  • Coarse stringy texture
Brown Slime Algae
Hincksia spp.
Algae

Fine brown fuzz. First thing to grow on new fouling. Forms a fuzzy coating.

How to Spot It

  • Fine brown filaments
  • Fuzzy appearance
  • Early coloniser - first to appear
  • Soft and delicate
Red Algae
Gracilaria & Gelidium spp.
Algae

Red/purple branching seaweed. Rubbery texture. Common on hulls.

How to Spot It

  • Red/purple colour
  • Branching fronds
  • Rubbery cartilage texture
  • Common in subtidal zone
Hairy Mussel
Trichomya hirsuta
Mussel

Native mussel with fuzzy hairy shell. Brown/black with distinctive bristly covering.

How to Spot It

  • Hairy/fuzzy shell covering
  • Brown/black colour
  • Dense clumps
  • Up to 60mm
